Why Your Story Matters

Lawmakers are bombarded with data, statistics, and lobbyists. But a single, authentic story can break through. When you share your loss, your struggle, or your family's pain, you become a voice that no spreadsheet can silence. Parents who have lost children, individuals who have battled addiction – your testimony is the most powerful tool we have.

“The most powerful weapon in politics is a personal story.” – Anonymous legislative aide
Persuasion Secrets of History's Greatest Legislators

Lyndon B. Johnson, Daniel Webster, and other legendary lawmakers understood that facts alone don't move people – stories do. Here are their techniques, adapted for your testimony.

🎯 1. The Johnson Treatment

LBJ would lean in close, make eye contact, and speak directly to a person's emotions. In your testimony, connect with each legislator as if they were sitting across from you. Use “you” and “your community” to make it personal.

📖 2. The Story Hook

Webster always opened with a vivid, human moment. Start with a single image: “I remember the last time I saw my son conscious...” That image stays in a legislator's mind.

⚖️ 3. The Moral Contrast

Lincoln contrasted right and wrong. Show the gap between what should be (safe communities) and what is (kratom sold like candy). Let them feel the injustice.

🔁 4. The Rule of Three

Cicero used triads for rhythm and memorability. For example: “This issue affects our children, our families, and our future.” It feels complete and persuasive.

Write Your Testimony: A Step‑by‑Step Guide
  • Step 1: Set the scene. Briefly introduce yourself and your connection to the issue. “My name is [Name], and I live in [City]. I'm here because my daughter almost died after buying kratom at a gas station.”
  • Step 2: Tell the story. Describe what happened in chronological order. Include sensory details – the call you received, the hospital waiting room, the helplessness. Keep it to 2–3 minutes when spoken.
  • Step 3: Connect to the legislation. Explain how the bill before them could prevent this from happening to another family. “This bill would remove these products from convenience stores, so no other parent gets that call.”
  • Step 4: State your ask clearly. “I urge you to vote YES on [Bill Number].” Be direct.
  • Step 5: End with a powerful closing. Repeat a key image or phrase. “Don't let another family experience the heartache we did.”
How to Refute Industry Claims When Testifying

During hearings, industry representatives will present carefully crafted arguments. You don't need to be an expert – just have a few key facts ready. Below are common industry claims and simple, truthful responses you can use. For more detail, refer legislators to our Evidence Library.

Industry ClaimSimple Refutation
“Kratom is just a plant – it's natural and safe.” “Many dangerous substances are natural. The issue is how the body processes it – kratom converts into a potent opioid-like compound once inside us. Poison control centers report thousands of exposures each year.”
“FDA denial was just about paperwork, not safety.” “The FDA explicitly said it could not even identify the ingredient, let alone conclude it was safe. That's a fundamental safety failure.”
“Deaths involving kratom are really caused by other drugs.” “Polydrug deaths don't erase the role of each substance. Many overdose deaths involve multiple substances – we don't ignore alcohol or cocaine when they're present. Kratom contributes to toxicity.”
“The KCPA would regulate kratom and make it safe.” “The KCPA sets labeling and age restrictions, but it doesn't address the fundamental pharmacology – the fact that the body turns it into a stronger opioid. Regulation is not the same as safety.”
“Studies show kratom is well tolerated.” “Those studies were short‑term, in healthy adults, using pure product – nothing like the gas station kratom that families encounter. Even in those studies, participants were withdrawn due to liver enzyme elevations and severe side effects.”
“Banning kratom will push people to fentanyl.” “That argument was used against restricting OxyContin in 2004. It didn't stop the opioid crisis – and it shouldn't stop us from removing a known danger from retail shelves.”
Pro tip: If you're asked a question you can't answer, it's okay to say: “I can't speak to that specific study, but I can direct you to our evidence library, which compiles the official FDA, CDC, and poison control data.”

“Good morning. I'm [Name], and I'm a recovering addict. My addiction started not with heroin, but with a legal substance called kratom, sold at a gas station near my college. I thought it was safe. I was wrong. It took me three years to break free. No one should have to go through that. This committee has the power to prevent others from falling into the same trap. Support this ban.”

👵 For a Grandparent

“My name is [Name], and I'm a grandmother from [City]. I never thought I'd be speaking about drug policy, but here I am. My grandson overdosed on kratom last year. He bought it at a convenience store – the same store where he buys gum. We were lucky; he survived. But the poison control center told us calls like ours are skyrocketing. Please, protect our grandchildren. Pass this bill.”

Tips for Delivering Your Testimony

In Person

  • Practice out loud – time yourself (most committees give 2‑3 minutes).
  • Bring printed copies for each committee member and staff.
  • Make eye contact, especially with the chair.
  • Pause for effect. Let silence work for you.
  • If you cry, that's okay. It's real.

Written Submission

  • Keep it to one page if possible.
  • Use bold for your “ask” so it stands out.
  • Include your contact information.
  • Attach any relevant evidence (photos, news clippings).
Pro tip from LBJ's staff: “If you can make them feel it in their gut, you've already won.”
