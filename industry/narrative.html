How the Industry Frames the Debate

In legislative testimony and policy memoranda, industry representatives frequently advance a consistent narrative about kratom safety, federal policy, and the role of newer kratom-derived products.

These claims often appear in testimony from the American Kratom Association and affiliated industry advocates.

Claim vs Evidence

Claim

“The FDA changed its position on kratom after a Single Ascending Dose study showed that kratom was well tolerated.”

Refute

A Single Ascending Dose (SAD) study evaluates short-term tolerance under controlled laboratory conditions. These studies are designed to determine pharmacokinetics and immediate adverse effects, not long-term safety, dependence potential, or population-level risk.

Regulatory policy is not determined by a single pharmacology study. Public-health agencies rely on broader surveillance systems including poison control data, mortality surveillance, adverse event reporting, and epidemiologic studies.

Claim

“The FDA has repeatedly tried to schedule kratom.”

Refute

Under U.S. law, the Food and Drug Administration does not schedule controlled substances. Scheduling authority rests with the Drug Enforcement Administration (DEA).

The FDA may provide scientific or medical evaluations to the DEA during scheduling reviews, but the final legal decision is made by the DEA under the Controlled Substances Act.

Claim

“The real issue is synthetic 7-hydroxymitragynine products, not kratom itself.”

Refute

Highly concentrated 7-hydroxymitragynine (7-OH) products have recently entered the market and raise serious safety concerns. However, this compound is derived directly from kratom alkaloids and is typically produced by chemically processing bulk kratom powder to increase the concentration of the active metabolite.

In other words, these products are not unrelated substances; they are concentrated derivatives of kratom’s primary alkaloids.

Claim

“Deaths and adverse events attributed to kratom are actually caused by newer 7-OH products.”

Refute

Most federal surveillance systems tracking kratom exposures — including poison center reports and mortality data — were collected years before high-potency 7-OH products appeared in the retail market.

Many of the datasets frequently cited in policy discussions therefore reflect exposures to traditional kratom products, not the newer concentrated derivatives.

Claim

“Products containing 7-hydroxymitragynine are different from kratom.”

Refute

7-hydroxymitragynine is a naturally occurring alkaloid and active metabolite of mitragynine, the primary compound found in kratom.

Manufacturers can increase its concentration through extraction and chemical processing of kratom plant material, creating products that are far more potent than the natural leaf.

For this reason, the distinction between “kratom” and “7-OH products” is largely a difference in concentration and formulation rather than an entirely separate substance.

Pro Tips for Legislative Testimony

1. Clarify Regulatory Authority

Explain that the DEA — not the FDA — has the authority to schedule controlled substances.

2. Distinguish Study Types

Point out that pharmacokinetic studies like Single Ascending Dose trials measure short-term tolerance and do not determine long-term safety or addiction risk.

3. Focus on Product Evolution

Highlight that concentrated alkaloid products such as 7-hydroxymitragynine extracts represent a newer form of the product with different potency characteristics.

4. Emphasize Surveillance Data

Most public-health datasets evaluating kratom harms were collected before the recent emergence of high-potency extracts.

5. Keep It Simple

Use a clear format when speaking: one claim, one correction, and one supporting fact.
