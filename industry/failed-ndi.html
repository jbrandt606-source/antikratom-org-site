The 75% Mitragynine Extract: A Bridge Too Far

In 2025, the industry submitted a New Dietary Ingredient (NDI) notification for a 75% mitragynine extract — a highly concentrated, standardized product that mimics a pharmaceutical, not a traditional botanical. The FDA reviewed the filing and issued a damning response: the ingredient’s identity was not established, safety data did not match the marketed product, and the submission was fundamentally incomplete.

“It is unclear how the product being marketed is qualitatively and quantitatively similar to the materials cited as evidence of safety.” — FDA response to TNT Manufacturing

This is not a minor technicality. The industry argued that because kratom leaf had “traditional use,” a 75% extract automatically qualifies as a supplement. The FDA rejected that logic. Without a valid NDI, any product containing this extract is considered adulterated under federal law, and interstate commerce is prohibited.

Incomplete Data, Evidentiary Gaps

The filing was missing full references, relied on unpublished abstracts, and failed to provide analytical linkage between the studied material and the final 75% extract. The FDA requested additional information, but the gaps remained. Key problems included:

  • ❌ No reliable method to ensure batch‑to‑batch consistency
  • ❌ Toxicological studies performed on different extracts — not the 75% formulation
  • ❌ No post‑market surveillance or adverse event plan
  • ❌ Unqualified safety claims given the mu‑opioid agonist activity

The FDA concluded that the evidence did not demonstrate reasonable certainty of no harm. For a dietary supplement, that’s a fatal verdict.

The Industry’s Own Warnings Condemn It

Inside the NDI proposal, the manufacturer listed “conditions of use” that read like a prescription label — an admission of danger:

  • ⚠️ Use limited to 28 days — not for long‑term consumption
  • ⚠️ Maximum 150 mg mitragynine per day (about 200 mg of extract)
  • ⚠️ Excludes pregnant/nursing women, people with liver disease, psychiatric conditions
  • ⚠️ Warning against combining with CNS depressants (alcohol, benzodiazepines, opioids)
  • ⚠️ Mandatory consultation with a healthcare provider before use

This is not the profile of a safe, unregulated botanical. It is the textbook definition of a high‑risk substance that requires medical oversight — yet the industry lobbies for gas‑station availability.

FDA Final Determination: Prohibited & Adulterated

In its formal objection letter, the FDA made clear that the NDI notification does not satisfy the requirements of 21 U.S.C. § 350b. Therefore, any dietary supplement containing this 75% mitragynine extract is adulterated under 21 U.S.C. § 342(f)(1)(B). Introduction into interstate commerce is prohibited.

🔥 “The notification does not provide an adequate basis to conclude that the dietary ingredient will reasonably be expected to be safe.” — FDA letter to Todd Underwood / TNT Manufacturing

This is a complete repudiation of the industry’s argument that kratom extracts are safe “dietary ingredients.” The FDA has drawn a clear line: concentrated mitragynine products cannot be lawfully marketed as supplements.
