Every legislative hearing arrives at the same moment. A witness stands before lawmakers and explains that kratom saved them. It treated their pain, anxiety, depression, opioid use disorder. It succeeded where doctors failed. The room nods sympathetically. But few stop to consider the extraordinary accusation hidden inside that testimony. Because if lawmakers truly believe these stories justify keeping kratom on store shelves, then they are also accepting a remarkable conclusion: that healthcare providers are so ineffective, so inaccessible, or so indifferent that patients have no choice but to seek treatment from a vape shop.

"Healthcare Failed Me" is a Vote of No Confidence

For decades, physicians, pharmacists, nurses, psychologists, addiction specialists, physical therapists, and pain specialists have devoted their careers to treating these conditions. Entire healthcare systems exist to manage chronic pain, depression, anxiety disorders, substance use disorders, and countless other medical conditions.

Yet every time a lawmaker repeats the claim that kratom is necessary because "it helps people when nothing else works," they are implicitly telling healthcare professionals that their work is inadequate. They are saying that a bag of unregulated powder sold next to energy drinks accomplishes what modern medicine cannot. They are saying that patients suffering from addiction are better served by a convenience store than by addiction specialists. They are saying that the healthcare system they oversee is so threadbare that desperate patients must turn to gas stations for relief.

You Cannot Have It Both Ways

Lawmakers cannot praise their state's healthcare infrastructure while simultaneously arguing that people must rely on products sold at convenience stores because medicine has failed them. Either healthcare works, or it does not. And if the argument for kratom is that patients have nowhere else to turn, then lawmakers should stop defending kratom and start asking why they have allowed a system to exist in which a gas station is viewed as a healthcare destination.

  • To physicians: Your residency, board exams, and clinical judgment are worth less than a bag of powder next to nicotine vapes.
  • To addiction specialists: We trust a convenience store employee more than your MAT protocols.
  • To pharmacists: Skip rigorous quality control; unregulated plant matter is fine.
  • To pain management experts: Your multimodal plans are obsolete; just buy unlabeled kratom capsules.

No physician should accept that narrative. No pharmacist should accept that narrative. No healthcare professional should accept being told that years of education, training, and evidence based practice are less valuable than whatever happens to be available behind the counter at a vape shop. And no lawmaker should accept it either.

The Double Standard: Autopsies for Harm, Selfies for Benefit

Kratom trade advocates routinely obtain autopsy reports, toxicology reports, death certificates, and medical examiner records through public records requests. They analyze them. They challenge them. They demand grieving families prove every detail. They demand forensic rigor when the outcome is harm.

But when a witness claims kratom treated their chronic pain, anxiety, depression, or opioid use disorder, the "evidence" is often a social media ad saved to camera roll at 2:35 AM. No physician notes. No treatment history. No objective outcome measures. Just a pink hippopotamus and an emotional testimony.

If lawmakers truly believe these stories justify keeping kratom on store shelves, then they should apply the same standard: every pro kratom witness should be required to submit medical documentation showing the diagnosis, prior treatment failures, and a licensed healthcare provider's note that kratom was the solution. Otherwise, the testimony is no different from a stuffed animal advertisement.

The Solution is Not a Vape Shop. It is Fixing Healthcare.

If patients cannot access effective treatment for pain, addiction, anxiety, or depression, then the solution is not to preserve an unregulated retail drug market. The solution is to improve healthcare access. If treatment is unavailable, expand treatment. If addiction care is inaccessible, fund addiction care. If pain management is inadequate, fix pain management. If mental health services are difficult to obtain, increase mental health services.

But do not tell healthcare professionals that the answer is a product sold in vape shops and gas stations. That is not a vote of confidence in patients. It is a vote of no confidence in the healthcare system.

Direct Message to Lawmakers

You are being asked to believe that a product promoted alongside "secret ingredient" emojis and plush toys outperforms surgeons, psychiatrists, and board certified pain doctors. That is an insult to every healthcare professional in your state.

  • Show the medical records, not the social media ads.
  • Show the physician's diagnosis and treatment failure.
  • Show the objective improvement under medical supervision.
  • Stop accepting IMG_7024.jpeg as clinical evidence.

If grieving families are expected to produce autopsies and toxicology reports, then pro kratom witnesses should be willing to submit medical documentation to the legislators, boards, and committees they are trying to influence. One standard. Not two.

The Anti-Kratom Association calls on medical boards to reject this insult. Demand that every pro kratom witness submit their full medical records to the legislative body, or stop pretending that healthcare has failed.

Stop the Insult. Invest in Real Healthcare.

Lawmakers: expand treatment. Fund addiction medicine. Strengthen pain management. Do not tell physicians they are incompetent while accepting social media ads as proof. Demand medical records, not camera roll screenshots taken at 2:35 AM.
