New York: 7-OH Bill Is Not a Ban – Demand Full Prohibition

New York lawmakers have approved legislation targeting high-concentration 7-hydroxymitragynine (7-OH) products. But this bill does not ban kratom. It leaves the vast majority of kratom products – powders, capsules, extracts – legally available in gas stations and vape shops. The only real solution is a full Schedule I ban on all kratom alkaloids.

WHY THE BILL FAILS KEY ORGANIZATIONS

New York's 7-OH Bill – A Narrow Limit, Not a Ban

In late June 2026, the New York Legislature passed a bill targeting high-concentration 7-hydroxymitragynine (7-OH) products. The bill now awaits Governor Hochul's signature. If signed into law, it would:

  • Prohibit the sale of products containing more than 2% 7-OH of total alkaloids or more than 1 mg of 7-OH per serving.
  • Apply to any substance, extract, mixture, or finished product that exceeds those limits.
  • Establish civil penalties for businesses that knowingly violate the law – up to $500 for a first violation and up to $1,000 for each subsequent violation, with funds directed to New York's Drug Treatment and Public Education Fund.
This is not a ban on kratom. It does not schedule kratom or 7-OH as controlled substances. It does not prohibit possession by consumers. It does not ban the vast majority of kratom products – only those exceeding the statutory 7-OH thresholds. Retailers can continue selling kratom powders, capsules, and extracts as long as they stay at or below 2% 7-OH and 1 mg per serving.

Why the 7-OH Bill Is a Dangerous Failure

  • It does not ban kratom. The bill only targets high-concentration 7-OH products. The vast majority of kratom products – powders, capsules, and extracts – remain completely legal. Gas stations and vape shops will continue selling kratom as before.
  • The 2% and 1 mg limits are arbitrary and unenforceable. There is no field test for 7-hydroxymitragynine. Law enforcement cannot verify compliance at the point of sale. Determining the limits requires expensive laboratory analysis that is not available to local police. The limits are political numbers, not real-world safety standards.
  • No mandatory testing for lead, ethanol, or contaminants. Independent labs have repeatedly found lead at unsafe levels and ethanol concentrations as high as 15-16% in liquid extracts – legally alcoholic. The 7-OH bill does not require testing for heavy metals, residual solvents, or biological contaminants. A product could be perfectly compliant with the 7-OH limits and still poison consumers.
  • It creates a dangerous loophole for high-mitragynine products. Because the bill regulates 7-OH concentration rather than total mitragynine content, manufacturers can formulate high-mitragynine products that comply with the numerical limits. Mitragynine itself is a potent opioid agonist with high abuse potential. This bill does nothing to limit mitragynine exposure.
  • It legitimizes an FDA-disputed substance. The FDA has repeatedly stated that kratom is not lawfully marketed as a dietary supplement and has no approved medical use. By creating a state-sanctioned regulatory pathway for compliant products, the bill gives kratom a government seal of approval, confusing consumers who assume "regulated" means "safe."
  • Civil penalties are meaningless. Fines of $500 to $1,000 per violation are trivial for an industry generating millions in revenue. There is no criminal penalty, no product seizure authority, and no meaningful enforcement mechanism. The bill is a paper tiger.
  • It does nothing to address addiction or overdose. The bill does not reduce access to kratom. It does not prevent addiction, withdrawal, or overdose. It simply moves the industry toward products that comply with the arbitrary limits while leaving the core public health crisis untouched.

Bottom line: The 7-OH bill is not public health policy. It is an industry-friendly trap designed to create the appearance of action while leaving kratom on shelves. The only honest answer is to reject this half-measure and instead pass a full Schedule I ban.

The Only Answer: Reject Half-Measures, Pass a Full Schedule I Ban

New York should not regulate kratom with arbitrary limits and civil fines. It should prohibit it – plain and simple. A full Schedule I ban removes kratom from every gas station, vape shop, and online retailer. No unenforceable caps, no false warnings, no loopholes for high-mitragynine products.

What to demand: "Reject the 7-OH bill and instead pass legislation that places all kratom alkaloids – mitragynine, 7-hydroxymitragynine, and all derivatives – under Schedule I. No exemptions, no caps, no civil penalties. Only a complete prohibition protects New York families."

If the legislature wants to address kratom, it must do so by ending its sale entirely – not by adding a cap that the industry can easily circumvent.
