Minnesota: Age 21 Law is Not Enough – Demand a Full Ban

HF 3453, passed in 2026, only raises the legal age for kratom possession from 18 to 21. It does not ban sales to adults, does nothing about contamination, and leaves an unregulated opioid on gas station shelves. Minnesota needs a complete Schedule I ban.

WHY THE LAW FAILS KEY ORGANIZATIONS

HF 3453 – A Minimal Age Bump, Not a Ban

In 2026, Minnesota passed HF 3453, which amends the state's controlled substances law to raise the legal age for possessing or selling kratom from 18 to 21. The law goes into effect August 1, 2026. That is all it does. It does not prohibit sale to adults. It does not require testing for lead or ethanol. It does not give law enforcement any new tools. It does not remove kratom from gas stations, vape shops, or online retailers.

A 21-age law is not a public health solution. It leaves the core problem untouched: an unregulated opioid that contains lead and alcohol, causes addiction and withdrawal, and has been linked to overdose deaths – still legally sold to anyone 21 and over.

Why the Minnesota Age-21 Law is a Deadly Failure

  • It does not ban kratom. Adults 21 and over can still buy powders, extracts, shots, and capsules at any gas station or vape shop. The law does nothing to reduce availability.
  • No contaminant testing required. Independent labs have found lead at levels exceeding safe daily intake and ethanol at 15-16% in liquid extracts (legally alcoholic). The new law does not require any pre-market or batch testing for heavy metals, residual solvents, or adulterants.
  • No enforcement mechanism. There is no field test to distinguish legal kratom from anything else. Law enforcement cannot verify alkaloid content or origin. The law has no practical way to be enforced.
  • Teens will still access it. Raising the age from 18 to 21 does not stop high school students from buying from older friends or online retailers that don't verify age. The law is a token gesture, not a barrier.
  • No medical use, no safety standard. The FDA has repeatedly warned that kratom has no approved medical use and is not safe as a dietary supplement. Minnesota's law implicitly legitimizes a dangerous product while doing nothing to address its pharmacology.
  • It ignores the real crisis. Kratom-related ER visits, poison center calls, and deaths have risen sharply nationwide. An age restriction does nothing to reduce those numbers – it simply moves the problem to slightly older users.

Bottom line: HF 3453 is a minimal, industry-friendly measure that creates the illusion of action. The only honest solution is a full Schedule I ban on all kratom products.

The Only Answer: Repeal HF 3453 and Pass a Full Schedule I Ban

Minnesota should not regulate kratom with age limits. It should prohibit it – plain and simple. A full Schedule I ban removes kratom from every gas station, vape shop, and online retailer. No age exceptions, no natural loopholes, no enforcement illusions.

What to demand: "Repeal HF 3453 – the age-21 kratom law. Instead, pass legislation that places all kratom alkaloids under Schedule I. Only a complete ban protects Minnesota families."

If the legislature wants to address kratom, it must do so by ending its sale entirely – not by pretending that raising the age from 18 to 21 makes a dangerous drug safe.

What You Can Do Right Now

  • Document retail sales in your community. Take photos of kratom displays in gas stations, vape shops, and stores near schools. Capture extracts, powders, shots, and any wellness or intoxication marketing. These images build a powerful record for lawmakers.
  • Build local awareness. Many Minnesotans don't know that the new law does almost nothing – or that kratom contains lead and alcohol. Share educational materials with parents, healthcare professionals, educators, local officials, and community groups.
  • Recruit stakeholders. Use the list below to connect with pharmacists, physicians, nurses, addiction professionals, law enforcement, educators, and parents. Ask each organization to support a full ban.
  • Follow developments in other states. Bans in states like Nevada and failures of KCPA bills elsewhere provide lessons and evidence for Minnesota lawmakers.
  • Prepare for the next session. Legislative action moves quickly. Build local networks, share educational materials, document retail activity, contact community leaders, and prepare testimony and evidence packets for lawmakers to demand repeal and replacement with a full ban.
