Wyoming: The KCPA is a Trap – Demand a Full Ban

In 2026, Wyoming passed SF0056 – a so‑called “Kratom Consumer Protection Act.” But this law is a regulatory illusion. It leaves gas‑station opioids on shelves, creates an unenforceable 2% cap, and does nothing to protect Wyoming families. The only answer is a complete Schedule I ban.

WHY THE KCPA FAILS → KEY ORGANIZATIONS

📜 SF0056 – A Regulatory Illusion, Not a Ban

Wyoming’s SF0056, signed into law in 2026, is marketed as “consumer protection.” But a close reading shows it’s an industry‑friendly framework that leaves dangerous kratom products on every gas station and vape shop shelf. The bill does not ban kratom. It merely slaps labels and age restrictions on an unregulated opioid.

🔥 The bill explicitly defines “kratom product” to EXCLUDE any synthesized or semi‑synthesized alkaloid – meaning the entire law applies only to “natural” products. But there is no field test to distinguish natural from synthetic. Law enforcement cannot enforce this distinction. The bill is unenforceable by design.

⛔ Why the Wyoming KCPA is a Deadly Failure

  • The “synthetic” loophole is a get‑out‑of‑jail‑free card. The bill only regulates “natural” kratom. Manufacturers can simply add a tiny amount of synthetic alkaloid and claim the product is now “synthetic” – exempt from the entire law. Wyoming has created a legal escape hatch, not a safety net.
  • The 2% 7‑OH cap is meaningless. The bill limits 7‑hydroxymitragynine to 2% of the alkaloid fraction. But manufacturers can easily bypass this by making larger tablets or capsules. A 500mg pill at 1.9% delivers the same amount of potent alkaloid as a 250mg pill at 3.8%. The cap is a political number, not a safety limit.
  • No field test – enforcement is a fantasy. There is no portable device that can distinguish “natural” from “synthetic” kratom at point of sale. Law enforcement officers cannot tell which products are legal and which are not. The bill appropriates $115,000 for testing – a laughably small amount for a state‑wide enforcement scheme that is impossible from the start.
  • It legalizes, not prohibits. The bill explicitly allows the sale of kratom to adults 21 and over, subject to labeling and age restrictions. Wyoming lawmakers have effectively legalized and normalized an unregulated opioid that has been found contaminated with lead and ethanol in independent lab tests. There is no warning on the label about addiction, withdrawal, or overdose risk.
  • Penalties are a slap on the wrist. A first offense is a misdemeanor with a maximum $1,000 fine – less than the profit from a single day of kratom sales at a busy smoke shop. Retailers will treat this as a cost of doing business, not a deterrent.
  • The “affirmative defense” clause protects bad actors. Retailers can avoid fines if they have a written policy against under‑21 sales and train their employees. No proof of actual compliance is required. This is a legal shield, not a consumer protection.
  • Contamination is not addressed. Independent lab tests have found lead at levels exceeding safe daily intake, and ethanol at concentrations as high as 15‑16% in liquid extracts (legally alcoholic). The KCPA does not require pre‑market testing or batch testing for heavy metals or residual solvents. “Natural” does not mean safe – and this bill does nothing to change that.

Bottom line: The Wyoming KCPA is not consumer protection. It is industry protection dressed up in regulatory language. It gives kratom a stamp of approval, makes enforcement impossible, and guarantees years of continued harm. The only honest answer is a complete Schedule I ban.

✅ The Only Answer: Repeal the KCPA and Pass a Full Ban

Wyoming should not regulate kratom. It should prohibit it – plain and simple. A full Schedule I ban removes kratom from every gas station, vape shop, and online retailer. No threshold games, no enforcement illusions.

📢 What to demand: “Repeal SF0056 – the Kratom Consumer Protection Act. Instead, pass legislation that places all kratom alkaloids under Schedule I. No age limits, no labeling, no “natural” exemptions. Only a complete ban protects public health.”

If the legislature wants to address kratom, it must do so by ending its sale entirely – not by creating a regulated market that is impossible to enforce and provides false reassurance to the public.
