Florida: KCPA is a Deadly Failure – Repeal and Ban

Florida leads the nation in kratom‑related deaths. Yet its Kratom Consumer Protection Act (KCPA) does nothing to stop the carnage. It leaves gas‑station opioids on shelves, imposes an unenforceable 2% cap, and provides no testing for lead or ethanol. The only answer is repeal and a full Schedule I ban.

Florida’s KCPA – A Regulatory Illusion, Not a Ban

Florida’s Kratom Consumer Protection Act (KCPA) was sold as a “consumer protection” law. In reality, it does not ban kratom. It sets an age limit of 21, caps 7‑hydroxymitragynine at 2% of the alkaloid fraction, and requires product registration and labeling. That is all. It does not require pre‑market testing for heavy metals, ethanol, or biological contaminants. It does not remove kratom from gas stations, vape shops, or online retailers. And it does nothing to address the fact that Florida has more kratom‑associated deaths than any other state.

A KCPA that tolerates lead and alcohol in products is not consumer protection. It is industry protection dressed up in paperwork. The law gives kratom a state‑approved stamp while Florida families continue to suffer.

Why the Florida KCPA is a Deadly Failure

  • Florida leads the nation in kratom‑related deaths. According to CDC and poison center data, Florida consistently reports the highest number of kratom-associated fatalities. The KCPA has not changed that trend.
  • The 2% 7‑OH cap is unenforceable. There is no field test for 7‑hydroxymitragynine. Law enforcement cannot verify compliance at the point of sale. Determining the cap requires laboratory analysis (LC‑MS/MS) that is expensive, slow, and not available to local police. The cap is a political number, not a real‑world safety limit.
  • No mandatory testing for lead, ethanol, or adulterants. Independent labs have repeatedly found lead at unsafe levels and ethanol concentrations as high as 15‑16% in liquid extracts – legally alcoholic. The Florida KCPA does not require testing for heavy metals, residual solvents, or biological contaminants. A product can be perfectly "registered" and still poison consumers.
  • Enforcement is virtually nonexistent. The law relies on complaint‑driven enforcement by the Department of Agriculture and Consumer Services, which has no expertise in drug toxicology. No dedicated inspectors, no proactive testing. Retailers face little risk of penalty.
  • It legitimizes an FDA‑disputed substance. The FDA has repeatedly stated that kratom is not lawfully marketed as a dietary supplement and has no approved medical use. The Florida KCPA creates a state‑sanctioned commercial pathway for a product the federal government still considers unsafe. Consumers assume “regulated” means “safe” – a deadly misconception.
  • No funding for real oversight. The law allows for registration fees, but those fees do not fund meaningful testing or inspection. In practice, the system is self‑certification by the industry. Processors submit their own certificates of analysis. Retailers rely on processor claims. There is no independent, proactive surveillance.
  • The 2% cap ignores the bigger problem. Mitragynine itself is an opioid agonist with high abuse potential. The law does nothing to limit mitragynine concentration or total dose. Manufacturers can simply increase serving size to deliver dangerous amounts of mitragynine while staying under the 7‑OH cap.

Bottom line: Florida’s KCPA is not consumer protection. It is a weak, industry‑friendly law that has failed to reduce deaths. The only honest answer is repeal and a full Schedule I ban.

The Only Answer: Repeal the KCPA and Pass a Full Schedule I Ban

Florida should not regulate kratom. It should prohibit it – plain and simple. A full Schedule I ban removes kratom from every gas station, vape shop, and online retailer. No 2% cap games, no enforcement by agriculture departments, no legitimization of a deadly drug.

What to demand: “Repeal the Florida Kratom Consumer Protection Act. Instead, pass legislation that places all kratom alkaloids under Schedule I. No registration, no labeling, no age exceptions. Only a complete ban protects Florida families.”

If the legislature wants to address kratom, it must do so by ending its sale entirely – not by creating a regulated market that has already failed to prevent the highest death rate in the nation.

  • Document retail sales in your community. Take photos of kratom displays in gas stations, vape shops, and stores near schools. Capture extracts, powders, shots, and any wellness or intoxication marketing. These images build a powerful record for lawmakers.
  • Build local awareness. Many Floridians don't know that the KCPA does almost nothing – or that Florida leads the nation in kratom deaths. Share educational materials with parents, healthcare professionals, educators, local officials, and community groups.
  • Recruit stakeholders. Use the list below to connect with pharmacists, physicians, nurses, addiction professionals, law enforcement, educators, and parents. Ask each organization to support repeal of the KCPA and a full ban.

  • Prepare for the next session. Legislative action moves quickly. Build local networks, share educational materials, document retail activity, contact community leaders, and prepare testimony and evidence packets for lawmakers to demand repeal and replacement with a full ban.
