Arizona: Amend to Full Schedule I Kratom Ban

Current proposals only target “synthetic” alkaloids – unenforceable and full of loopholes. We need an amendment to place all kratom alkaloids (natural & synthetic) under Schedule I.

❌ Current “Synthetic‑Only” Bills Are Unenforceable

Arizona is considering bills that only restrict “synthetic” or “semi‑synthetic” kratom alkaloids. That approach is fatally flawed because:

  • There is no field test to distinguish synthetic from natural mitragynine or 7‑hydroxymitragynine at the point of sale.
  • No real‑time verification – retailers, law enforcement, and regulators cannot tell which products are illegal and which are “exempt.”
  • Same packaging, same labels, same stores – the distinction is meaningless in commerce.
🚨 A “synthetic‑only” ban is not a ban – it’s a loophole. All dangerous kratom products – lead‑contaminated powders, high‑ethanol extracts, potent shots – look identical. Leaving “natural” kratom legal leaves the public health crisis untouched.

✅ The Only Solution: Amend to Full Schedule I for ALL Kratom Alkaloids

We need a clean amendment that places every kratom alkaloid – mitragynine, 7‑hydroxymitragynine, and any derivative – into Schedule I of Arizona’s controlled substances list.

🎯 Your exact ask to every legislator:
“Amend the current kratom bill to include ALL kratom alkaloids under Schedule I – not just synthetic versions. Without this change, the bill is unenforceable and leaves deadly products on gas station shelves.”

A full Schedule I ban is supported by:

  • ✅ The FDA’s position – kratom has no accepted medical use and high abuse potential.
  • ✅ Contamination evidence – independent labs have found lead, ethanol, and other toxins in natural kratom products.
  • ✅ Unprecedented variability – alkaloid content varies 10‑fold, making overdose inevitable.
  • ✅ Expert boards – other states’ Boards of Pharmacy have already concluded that all kratom alkaloids meet Schedule I criteria.

🔬 Why “Natural” vs “Synthetic” Cannot Be Enforced

Proponents of a synthetic‑only ban often claim they are targeting only the most dangerous products. But in the real world:

  • No portable testing device exists that can identify mitragynine’s origin.
  • Mass spectrometry – the only reliable method – is expensive and not available to local law enforcement.
  • Retailers will simply relabel products as “100% natural extract” and continue selling.
⚠️ Result: The bill would create the illusion of action while leaving the exact same products available at the exact same stores. No health agency can enforce a distinction they cannot measure.
