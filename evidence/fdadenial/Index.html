FDA Denial of Johnson Foods Kratom NDI

Two core documents matter when policymakers consider whether kratom should remain a retail product: (1) the New Dietary Ingredient (NDI) submission filed by Johnson Foods and (2) the FDA denial letter explaining why the agency could not conclude the ingredient would be safe.

View the PDFs Why this matters
The two PDFs in this folder

1) The NDI submission (the company’s application)

This is Johnson Foods’ formal New Dietary Ingredient notification for “NPI-001, a dried kratom leaf powder,” prepared for FDA’s Center for Food Safety and Applied Nutrition. It describes the ingredient and the company’s proposed conditions of use. [oai_citation:2‡NDI Johnson foods Kratom.pdf](sediment://file_00000000889471f790dfc343416dde35)

  • Identifies the ingredient as dried Mitragyna speciosa leaf powder (“kratom”). [oai_citation:3‡NDI Johnson foods Kratom.pdf](sediment://file_00000000889471f790dfc343416dde35)
  • States it is an NDI because the sponsor is not presenting evidence it was marketed in the U.S. before Oct 15, 1994. [oai_citation:4‡NDI Johnson foods Kratom.pdf](sediment://file_00000000889471f790dfc343416dde35)
  • Proposes a serving size of 50 mg/day with intermittent use limits (15 days on / 15 days off). [oai_citation:5‡NDI Johnson foods Kratom.pdf](sediment://file_00000000889471f790dfc343416dde35)

2) The FDA denial letter (the government’s response)

This is FDA’s letter (dated January 4, 2023) responding to the NDI filing. FDA explains why it could not conclude the product would “reasonably be expected to be safe” under the proposed conditions of use. [oai_citation:6‡FDA Johnson foods letter.pdf](sediment://file_00000000fac071f5a13a78e2727b87ab)

  • FDA confirms the NDI was received and filed on Oct 21, 2022 and describes the proposed use conditions. [oai_citation:7‡FDA Johnson foods letter.pdf](sediment://file_00000000fac071f5a13a78e2727b87ab)
  • FDA states it could not establish the identity and could not establish the safety of the ingredient from the submission. [oai_citation:8‡FDA Johnson foods letter.pdf](sediment://file_00000000fac071f5a13a78e2727b87ab)
  • FDA warns that a product with this NDI may be considered adulterated under federal law if safety is not established. [oai_citation:9‡FDA Johnson foods letter.pdf](sediment://file_00000000fac071f5a13a78e2727b87ab)

Why the FDA denial matters for a retail ban
Plain-language summary

Johnson Foods attempted to position whole-leaf kratom powder as a “new dietary ingredient” with a labeled serving size of 50 mg/day and intermittent use limits. FDA responded that the submission did not provide an adequate basis to conclude the ingredient would be safe under the proposed conditions.

Identity problems

FDA states it was unable to establish the identity of the NDI based on the information provided, which undermines any safety conclusion built on comparisons to other products or literature. [oai_citation:10‡FDA Johnson foods letter.pdf](sediment://file_00000000fac071f5a13a78e2727b87ab)

Safety concerns

FDA describes concerns about adverse effects from prolonged consumption—tolerance, physical dependence, abuse/addiction potential and withdrawal, neurotoxicity/memory deficits, drug–drug interactions, and even death. [oai_citation:11‡FDA Johnson foods letter.pdf](sediment://file_00000000fac071f5a13a78e2727b87ab)

Policy impact

FDA states that a product containing this NDI may be considered adulterated if there is inadequate information to provide reasonable assurance it does not present a significant or unreasonable risk. [oai_citation:12‡FDA Johnson foods letter.pdf](sediment://file_00000000fac071f5a13a78e2727b87ab)

Why lawmakers care
  • The “dietary supplement” pathway failed here. The sponsor tried to use the NDI process; FDA did not accept the safety case. [oai_citation:13‡FDA Johnson foods letter.pdf](sediment://file_00000000fac071f5a13a78e2727b87ab)
  • It supports retail removal. If federal regulators cannot conclude “reasonable expectation of safety,” states have a strong basis to restrict or ban retail availability as a consumer protection measure. [oai_citation:14‡FDA Johnson foods letter.pdf](sediment://file_00000000fac071f5a13a78e2727b87ab)
  • It shifts the burden of proof. The company’s own submission acknowledges it is an NDI (not grandfathered pre-1994), so the burden is on the manufacturer to prove safety before market normalization.
